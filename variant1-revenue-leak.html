The Structural Failure

Where the Cash Is Actually Going

The problem is not that your business lacks data. You have more data than you can process. The problem is that the data is backward-looking by the time it reaches your desk.

01

The Manual Extraction Loop

Your controller runs scheduled exports from your ERP, pastes them into Excel, applies formula layers built over years of tribal knowledge, and distributes a report that was already stale before the export ran. This is not a one-time event. It is a recurring ritual that costs 8 to 15 hours of skilled finance time per close cycle and introduces cumulative formula errors that surface only when reconciliation fails.

02

The AR Aging Silent Drain

Customers paying at net-75 while contracted at net-45 are borrowing working capital from your business at zero interest. When this pattern exists across 15 or 20 customers simultaneously, the aggregate working capital drain can reach seven figures annually without ever appearing as a distinct line item in your P&L. It shows up only as a cash shortfall you did not anticipate.

03

Margin Compression You Learn About Late

A raw materials cost spike hits in week one of the month. Your P&L review happens in week six after close. Every job quoted and executed during that window was priced against the wrong cost basis. At $50M in revenue, even a 2 percent margin compression sustained for four to six weeks represents $400,000 to $600,000 in lost gross profit that your reporting never flagged in real time.

04

The Single Point of Failure Problem

The Excel reporting model your finance team built over the last three years is maintained by one person. Its logic is undocumented. The formulas reference pivot tables that break when source column headers change after an ERP update. When that person resigns, so does your financial intelligence infrastructure. Most mid-market businesses are one key departure away from a month-end reporting crisis.
